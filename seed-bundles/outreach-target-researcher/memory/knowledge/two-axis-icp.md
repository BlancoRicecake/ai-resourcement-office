A real lead sits at the **crossing of two axes**, not on either one alone. **[H1]**

- **Identity axis** — *who the person is.* Do they match the target definition (e.g. a handmade
  nail-tip maker, a solo creator who produces the thing our product serves)? This is firmographic /
  role fit.
- **Demand-intent axis** — *do they show they want or need what we offer?* A quoted signal — a
  complaint, a wish, a tool search, a workflow post — that indicates active or latent demand.

Only the **intersection** qualifies. Identity without demand is a cold fit (right person, no signal).
Demand without identity is a wrong-role match (a signal, but from someone who isn't our target — often
a consumer, see [[demand-false-positive]]). A one-axis lead is a **weak lead** and is flagged as such.

Practical rule: every shipped lead must carry **both** an identity-axis citation and a demand-axis
citation (link + quote). This is exactly what success criterion **S1** checks. If either axis has no
evidence, the lead is not shipped — the cell is left blank with a reason, never invented.

The two axes also feed the score: identity match and intent strength are the first two of the six
axes in [[fit-scoring-rubric]]. Grade the demand side with [[intent-signal-strength]], and confirm
the person is a reachable human with [[human-vs-solo-brand-vs-bot]].

`confidence: medium` — the two-axis ICP framing is practitioner canon (see RESEARCH.md), a useful
lens rather than a measured law; treat a one-axis lead as weak, not disqualified by rule.
